The Project Idea

The intent of the project is to compare two articles regarding Monet's colors used in the making of the "Water Lilies," respectively Monet's Palette in the Twentieth Century: "Water-Lilies" and "Irises" and Color, Chemistry, and Creativity in Monet’s Water Lilies, and identify their similarities and differences through the creation of an ontology and the implementation of competency questions based on it; these can then be verified by querying them in SPARQL language. The first article refers to the artworks preserved at the National Gallery in London from a historical perspective and then focuses on detailed analyses of the specific color tones including their chemical compositions used in both Water Lilies 1916 and Irises 1914-1917. The second article, on the other hand, refers to works preserved at the Art Institute of Chicago and reviews the color palette used by Monet for the 1906 Water Lilies by identifying its historical and color composition components through a scientific analysis.

Informal Competency Questions

The first step concerned the modeling of the source texts by the creation of competency questions obtained from the sources to identify the most relevant information for the purposes of our research. Three groups were considered: the first refers specifically to the artworks of the National Gallery, the second to those of the Art Institute, and the third aims to compare the two texts through comparison questions. An expected result was reported for each question in order to verify the reliability of the SPARQL queries that will be seen later.

The National Gallery

Read me

● What is the work of art observed? Water Lilies (1916), Irises (1914-17)
● What are the main historical sources reporting the use of the mentioned colors? The main are: Moisse’list and Letter
● Does the information obtained from the laboratory conflict with what is reported in the historical sources? A range of samples used to infer the full palette of Water Lilies is similar to Moisse's list
● What do Water Lilies and Irises have in common and what are the differences? In common: Luminous white grounds. Differences: Irises has two layers of ground; Water Lilies has the use of two binders
● Which pigment is most commonly used in the Water Lilies series? Cobalt blue

The Art Institute Of Chicago

Read me

● What is the observed work of art? Water Lilies (1906)
● Recover 19th century advances that influenced the way art was made: production of new pigments, invention of the metal paint tube, new equipment
● Recover the difference between French ultramarine and cobalt blue: 1. typically warmer, reddish blue; 2. appears cooler and more delicate
● How did Monet create one of the red flowers? By combining red lake with vermilion

Comparison Questions

The Ontology

Resource modeling is continued by defining classes and properties through the manual creation of an ontology using Protégé software, MOC, which relates the texts. Next, the ontology was serialized in turtle syntax and can be viewed interactively through a web application. In addition, significant individuals were identified for the realization of the model and the links between them can be observed in the knowledge graph; thanks to these two visualization methods, users can explore the MOC ontology in a complete and in-depth manner, understanding how the concepts and relationships that characterize the domain of reference of the project have been modeled. Both turtle serialization and knowledge graph can be downloaded.

SPARQL: Formal Competency Questions

The last phase involved the translation of the competency questions into informal questions answered by SPARQL queries that can be tested through the provided python codes. The main PREFIXs considered are given below
PREFIX moc:
PREFIX owl:
PREFIX rdf:
PREFIX rdfs:

What is the work of art observed?

QUERY
SELECT ?artworkTitle
WHERE {
?artwork moc:heldIn moc:The_National_Gallery ;
moc:hasTitle ?artworkTitle .
}

What are the main historical sources reporting the use of the mentioned colors?

QUERY
SELECT ?typeObject
WHERE {
{?typeObject rdf:type moc:TypeObject ;
rdfs:label "Moisse's list"@en .
}
UNION
{?typeObject rdf:type moc:TypeObject ;
rdfs:label "letter"@en .
}
}

Does the information obtained from the laboratory conflict with what is reported in the historical sources?

QUERY
SELECT ?ArtTool ?isSimilarTo
WHERE {
moc:sample moc:isTestedFor ?ArtTool .
moc:sample moc:isSimilarTo ?isSimilarTo .
}

What do Water Lilies and Irises have in common and what are the differences?

QUERY
SELECT ?ArtDetail
WHERE {
{
moc:Irises moc:hasDetail ?ArtDetail .
}
UNION
{
moc:WaterLilies moc:hasDetail ?ArtDetail .
}
}

Which pigment is most commonly used in the Water Lilies series?

QUERY
SELECT ?SpecificColor
WHERE {
?SpecificColor moc:mainElementOf moc:WaterLilies .
?SpecificColor rdf:type moc:SpecificColor .
}

What is the observed work of art?

QUERY
SELECT ?artworkTitle
WHERE {
?artwork moc:heldIn moc:The_Art_Institute_of_Chicago ;
moc:hasTitle ?artworkTitle .
}

Recover 19th century advances that influenced the way art was made

QUERY
SELECT ?advances
WHERE {
?event rdf:type moc:Event ;
rdfs:label "Changes in the art field"@en ;
moc:hasEvent ?advances .
FILTER (?advances IN (moc:new_pigments, moc:metal_paint_tube, moc:new_equipment))
}

Recover the difference between French ultramarine and cobalt blue

QUERY
SELECT ?french_prop ?cobalt_prop
WHERE {
?french a moc:SpecificColor ;
rdfs:label "French ultramarine"@en ;
moc:hasProperty ?french_prop .
?cobalt a moc:SpecificColor ;
rdfs:label "Cobalt blue"@en ;
moc:hasProperty ?cobalt_prop .
}

How did Monet create one of the red flowers?

QUERY
SELECT ?color1 ?color2
WHERE {
?waterlilies rdf:type moc:ArtWorkTitle ;
moc:hasArtist moc:Monet ;
moc:hasDetail ?redflowers .
?redflowers rdf:type moc:ArtDetail ;
moc:hasColor ?color1, ?color2 .
FILTER (?color1 = moc:Vermillion && ?color2 = moc:Red_lake)
}

Retrieve the main goals of the papers

QUERY
SELECT ?title ?typegoal
WHERE {
?title rdf:type moc:TitleObject ;
rdfs:label ?label ;
moc:hasTypeGoal ?typegoal .

FILTER(regex(str(?label), "Color, Chemistry, and Creativity in Monet's Water Lilies|Monet's Palette in the Twentieth Century: Water-lilies and Irises"))
}

What are the methods applied by laboratories to analyze colors from the paintings themselves?

QUERY
SELECT ?Museum ?LabTechnique
WHERE {
?museum moc:hasTechnique ?technique .
?museum rdf:type moc:Museum .
?museum rdfs:label ?Museum .
?technique rdfs:label ?LabTechnique .
}

Retrieve the palettes of Water Lilies and the colors in common

QUERY
SELECT ?palette ?SpecificColor
WHERE {
{
moc:palette1906 moc:hasColor ?specificColor .
?specificColor rdfs:label ?SpecificColor .
moc:palette1906 rdfs:label ?palette .
}
UNION
{
moc:palette1916 moc:hasColor ?specificColor .
?specificColor rdfs:label ?SpecificColor .
moc:palette1916 rdfs:label ?palette .
}
}

SELECT ?color
WHERE {
?palette1 rdf:type moc:ArtTool .
?palette2 rdf:type moc:ArtTool .
?palette1 moc:hasColor ?color .
?palette2 moc:hasColor ?color .
FILTER (?palette1 != ?palette2)
}
GROUP BY ?color
HAVING (COUNT(DISTINCT ?palette1) > 1)

What is the original name of the color viridian according to the articles?

QUERY
SELECT ?name
WHERE {
moc:Viridian moc:hasName ?name .
}

What techniques used Monet to make the Water Lilies?

QUERY
SELECT DISTINCT ?technique
WHERE {
?waterlilies rdf:type moc:ArtWorkTitle ;
moc:hasArtist moc:Monet ;
moc:hasTechnique ?technique .
?technique rdf:type moc:ArtTechnique .
}

Conclusion

Our research focused on the comparison of two descriptive texts of the same cultural topic and their interoperability represented in the knowledge graph. Similarities and differences were analyzed with regard to the subject matter then tested by means of competency questions and, in a second step, computationally verified informal questions. For the purposes of the project, several tools made available for the purpose were useful, among the first being the Protégé software and followed by easily searchable online applications. The ontology created for the purpose can be easily enriched with other resources concerning Monet, classifying his artworks based on the predominant color tone, and/or the field of contemporary art to enable its usefulness to the relation of other items. Suggestions are made regarding the art current of Impressionism including techniques and other artists. The subclass regarding art movements, however allows for the addition of other currents and thus an expansion of the ontology itself. Moreover, MOC could be used as a tool for analyzing and cataloging existing artworks, allowing for more accurate and efficient identification and classification of the colors used by various artists.

The MOC ontology was designed to be user-friendly for experts in art history and computer science, as well as for fans of Monet and his masterpieces.
